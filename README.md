In this lesson, we identify the tonic, dominant, and subdominant in one key, but across several different octaves.

The lesson consists of 12 exercises.  
In the first four exercises, the lower note is the first degree <span class="text-h4" style="color: rgb(0,128,128)">❶</span>  
In the next two (5 and 6), the lower note is the fifth degree, one octave higher <span class="text-h4" style="color: rgb(0,130,200)">❺⬆️</span>  
In the next two (7 and 8), the lower note is the fourth degree, one octave higher <span class="text-h4" style="color: rgb(240,50,230)">❹⬆️</span>  
In the next two (9 and 10), the lower note is the fifth degree, one octave lower <span class="text-h4" style="color: rgb(0,130,200)">❺⬇️</span>  
And in the final two (11 and 12), the lower note is the fourth degree, one octave lower <span class="text-h4" style="color: rgb(240,50,230)">❹⬇️</span>

So in total, we cover more than two octaves within a single key.

Try taking this lesson by listening to the tonic only in the first exercise. Then switch the reference option to “None” so that you complete all the remaining exercises from memory.
